Hello, I'm

Furkan Ercan.

I was born in Turkey on 1988. I hold a B.Sc. degree in Electrical and Electronics Engineering, and an M.Sc. degree in Sustainable Environment and Energy Systems, both from Middle East Technical University (METU), Northern Cyprus Campus . My B.Sc./M.Sc. advisor was Prof. Dr. Ali Muhtaroğlu. I have received my Ph.D. degree in Electrical and Computer Engineering at McGill University. My Ph.D. advisor was Prof. Warren J. Gross .

I am currently affiliated as a Postdoctoral Associate at the Wireless Integrated Systems and Extreme Circuits (WISE-Circuits) lab at Boston University, working with Prof. Rabia Tuğçe Yazıcıgil. Our work is in collaboration with Prof. Muriel Médard from MIT and Prof. Ken Duffy from Maynooth University.

My research focus is primarily on Guessing Random Additive Noise Decoding, or GRAND in short, which is a recent universal decoding algorithm that works with any existing channel coding scheme on the planet. Its capacity-achieving properties and super-lightweight processing make it an attractive solution to many existing and emerging applications, including the use cases in 5G wireless communications and beyond. Like many interesting topics on Information Theory, GRAND needs to be translated from theory into practice. So part of my research interest is about translating GRAND algorithm into realization. Another research focus carried over from my Ph.D. is on Polar Codes, a channel coding scheme which quickly made its way to the 5G wireless communication standard due to its attractive properties. I enjoy developing decoder algorithms, realize them in software and implement in hardware. I also take joy in trying to explain how decoding algorithms work in theory.

On the industrial side, from 2020 to 2021, I have worked as a 5G Physical Layer Algorithm Developer at Octasic Inc. I was involved in the Algo team, where I work on design and optimization of 5G wireless protocol systems. From 2011 to 2012, I worked at Intel Labs in OR, USA as a graduate research intern, where I created a system-level dynamic energy management policy using Intel server architectures.
